## Abstract

Laser range-gated imaging can provide depth-related observations beyond a single two-dimensional intensity image, which is potentially useful for discriminating true 3D targets from planar false targets. However, classifiers trained on simulated gated images may exploit non-physical shortcuts such as black frames, absolute brightness differences, or rendering artifacts. This study presents a physics-interpretable Blender-based gated-imaging simulation and anti-shortcut validation framework for true 3D military target versus planar false-target discrimination. True targets are rendered as three-gate image stacks with visible depth-dependent responses, while planar false targets are generated by camera-depth flattening and rectangular pulse-gate overlap modulation. The framework includes scalar shortcut diagnosis, per-gate maximum normalization, model-level grouped validation, single-gate ablations, structured nuisance tests, and domain-randomized training. Experiments on 44 manually selected military 3D models show that the raw simulation contains a strong scalar shortcut, with gate-2 p99 intensity reaching 0.9886 threshold accuracy. Per-gate maximum normalization reduces the strongest scalar shortcut to 0.7955. On a four-view grouped validation dataset, the full three-gate stack achieves mean accuracies of 0.9848, 0.9811, and 0.9205 under clean, light-noise, and strong-noise evaluation, respectively. Clean/noisy-trained models fail under structured hard-nuisance shifts, whereas domain-mixed training improves the robustness boundary and reaches 0.9394, 0.9242, 0.9091, 0.9394, and 0.7727 under clean, light-noise, strong-noise, hard-mild, and hard-strong evaluation. These results support gate-stack validation in controlled simulation while emphasizing the need for shortcut diagnostics before robustness claims.

**Keywords:** laser gated imaging; range-gated imaging; planar false target; 3D target discrimination; Blender simulation; anti-shortcut validation; domain randomization

## 1. Introduction

Target recognition under active deception remains difficult when a planar false target visually resembles a real object from a given viewpoint. Conventional two-dimensional image recognition mainly depends on texture, contour, and local appearance cues. When the false target preserves a similar visible silhouette, a single image may not provide enough evidence to determine whether the object has real three-dimensional structure.

Laser range-gated imaging offers a physically different observation mechanism. A transmitted laser pulse illuminates the scene, while a receiver gate selects echoes within a controlled time window. Different gates correspond to different range intervals, so a gated image stack contains both spatial appearance and range-dependent response information. For a real 3D target, visible surfaces occupy a range of depths and therefore contribute differently to different gates. For a planar false target located near one depth plane, the visible silhouette should remain more consistent across nonzero gates, with intensity changes governed by the pulse-gate temporal overlap.

The central challenge in simulation-based validation is that a neural network may solve the task for the wrong reason. For example, if a false target appears in only one gate while the other gates are black, a classifier can detect black frames rather than depth structure. If true and false classes differ systematically in brightness, a scalar feature such as maximum intensity or p99 intensity may classify the data without using the intended multi-gate cue. Therefore, simulated gated-image classification requires explicit anti-shortcut validation before accuracy can be interpreted as physical evidence.

This work addresses the following question:

```text
Can a physically interpretable three-gate image stack provide more stable evidence than any individual gate for discriminating true 3D targets from planar false targets, after major simulation shortcuts are diagnosed and controlled?
```

The main contributions are:

1. A Blender-based gated-imaging simulation pipeline for selected military 3D models and corresponding planar false targets.
2. A rectangular pulse-gate overlap model for generating interpretable true 3D and planar false-target gate responses.
3. An anti-shortcut validation protocol including scalar shortcut diagnosis, per-gate normalization, grouped validation, single-gate ablations, and independent noise evaluation.
4. A four-view grouped validation experiment showing that the full three-gate stack outperforms single-gate baselines on average under clean, light-noise, and strong-noise evaluation.
5. A hard-nuisance boundary and domain-randomized training analysis showing that structured nuisance variation must be included in the simulation domain before robustness claims are made.

The work is positioned as a simulation and validation framework, not as a deployment-ready military recognition system and not as a new neural architecture.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Laser range-gated imaging

Laser range-gated imaging uses active pulsed illumination and temporally controlled receiver gates to suppress out-of-gate background and encode range-dependent information. In three-dimensional laser gated range-intensity correlation imaging, the relationship between transmitted pulse, receiver gate, echo delay, and received intensity can be modeled through range-intensity profiles. Rectangular pulse and gate windows naturally produce triangular or trapezoidal response profiles depending on their relative widths [1]. This physical principle motivates the gate-response model used in the present simulation.

### 2.2 Learning-based gated imaging and depth priors

Recent learning-based 3D range-gated imaging studies use gate stacks for dense depth estimation. Transformer-based methods and adaptive depth-bin strategies show that gated images can provide structured optical depth priors rather than ordinary texture channels [2]. These works motivate treating gate images as physically related observations. However, the present task is different: instead of reconstructing dense depth, this study evaluates whether gate-stack observations can discriminate true 3D targets from planar false targets while avoiding simulation shortcuts.

### 2.3 Gated cameras and multimodal perception

Gated cameras have been used in robust perception systems, especially under adverse visibility, low-light, or weather-degraded conditions. In multimodal object detection, gated near-infrared images can complement RGB, LiDAR, and radar [3]. Recent diffractive optical imaging systems also suggest longer-term routes for optical-neural image formation and projection [4]. This literature motivates the long-term direction of combining gated imaging with multimodal or optical-neural recognition. The present work provides a narrower prerequisite: a controlled simulation and validation protocol for one physically meaningful discrimination task.

### 2.4 Shortcut learning and simulation-to-real risk

Shortcut learning is a general risk in deep learning: models may exploit cues that correlate with labels in the training data but do not reflect the intended causal or physical mechanism [5]. Synthetic optical imaging adds another risk because simulator artifacts may be easier to learn than real physical structure. Domain randomization is one way to reduce this risk by exposing the model to controlled variation during training [6]. In this paper, shortcut diagnostics and domain-randomized simulation are treated as necessary components of the validation chain.

## 3. Physical Model and Simulation

### 3.1 Rectangular pulse-gate overlap

Let the transmitted pulse be a rectangular temporal window of width \(T_l\), and let the receiver gate be another rectangular temporal window of width \(T_g\). If the echo from a surface point arrives at time \(\tau\) and the center of gate \(i\) is \(c_i\), the normalized gate response is modeled as the normalized overlap length:

\[
W_i(\tau)=
\frac{\left|\mathrm{rect}_{T_l}(t-\tau)\cap \mathrm{rect}_{T_g}(t-c_i)\right|}
{\min(T_l,T_g)}.
\]

When \(T_l=T_g\), the response profile is triangular. When one window is wider than the other, the response becomes trapezoidal with a plateau. In the current v8 simulation setting, the receiver gate is wider than the laser pulse, so a trapezoidal response is used.

Figure 2. Rectangular pulse-gate overlap model used in the gated-image simulation. The normalized received response is determined by the temporal overlap length between the transmitted laser pulse and the receiver gate. Equal pulse/gate widths produce a triangular response, whereas the v8 unequal-width setting produces a trapezoidal response with a plateau. A planar false target samples the same whole-object response across gates, while a true 3D target integrates responses from multiple visible depths. Source file: `writing/figures/fig2_rectangular_overlap_response.png`.

### 3.2 True 3D target gate-stack generation

For a true 3D target, visible surface points have different camera depths. The \(i\)-th gate image is modeled as:

\[
I_i(x,y)=R(x,y)W_i(\tau(x,y)),
\]

where \(R(x,y)\) is the rendered visible reflectance or intensity and \(\tau(x,y)\) is the depth-dependent echo delay. Because \(\tau(x,y)\) varies over the object surface, different gates emphasize different depth regions. Blender is used to load and render each selected military 3D model, producing a three-gate grayscale stack:

\[
X=[I_0,I_1,I_2].
\]

### 3.3 Planar false-target construction

The planar false target is generated by flattening the target geometry along the camera-depth direction while preserving the visible silhouette. This construction removes the target's physical depth distribution but avoids the unrealistic behavior of showing only a partial object in each gate. The false target is then modulated by the same rectangular pulse-gate response. Thus, a planar false target should appear as a whole-object silhouette across nonzero gates, with gate intensity controlled by pulse-gate overlap rather than by an arbitrary manual fade.

Figure 3. Representative raw gate-stack images generated by the v8 rectangular-overlap simulation. The true 3D target changes its visible structure across gates because different depth regions contribute to different range windows. In contrast, the planar false target preserves the complete object silhouette across gates, with intensity changes governed by the pulse-gate overlap response. Source file: `writing/figures/fig3_true3d_flatfalse_gate_examples.png`.

## 4. Dataset and Anti-Shortcut Protocol

### 4.1 Dataset

The study uses 44 manually selected military 3D models. The selected source models include 12 main battle tank models, 20 fighter jet models, and 12 attack helicopter models. Each model is rendered as a true 3D target and paired with a corresponding planar false target. The single-view dataset contains 88 samples and 264 gate images. The four-view dataset renders each source model under four yaw angles (0, 90, 180, and 270 degrees), producing 352 samples and 1056 gate images.

For the four-view dataset, validation splitting is grouped by the underlying source model. All views and domain variants of the same source object are assigned to the same split partition. This prevents cross-view leakage.

### 4.2 Scalar shortcut diagnosis

Before neural training, scalar single-gate features are tested with threshold classifiers. The raw v8 dataset contains a strong shortcut: gate 2 p99 intensity reaches 0.9886 threshold accuracy. This means that the raw dataset cannot be used as direct evidence that the classifier learns depth-gated structure.

Per-gate maximum normalization is applied to reduce absolute brightness differences. After normalization, the strongest scalar shortcut decreases to 0.7955 and shifts mainly toward edge/shape statistics. This does not eliminate all single-gate information, but it weakens the dominant brightness shortcut and motivates formal single-gate neural ablations.

## 5. Neural Baseline and Training Setup

The neural model is a lightweight electronic baseline used to test whether the simulated gate stacks contain discriminative information. It is not the main innovation. A shared CNN encoder extracts features from each gate. The full-stack setting fuses all three gate features, while the single-gate baselines use only one selected gate. Fusion modes include mean fusion, attention fusion, and attention-residual fusion. The classifier head outputs logits for two classes, `true3d` and `flat_false`; softmax probabilities are used only for interpretation, while cross-entropy loss is applied to logits during training.

Main experiments use three random seeds:

```text
42 / 332 / 2026
```

Independent evaluation conditions are:

| condition | gaussian noise std | poisson peak | background scatter |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| clean | 0.00 | 0 | 0.00 |
| light noise | 0.02 | 80 | 0.02 |
| strong noise | 0.05 | 30 | 0.05 |

Structured hard-nuisance evaluation additionally introduces low-frequency reflectance variation, weak background scatter, and partial occlusion. Domain-randomized training mixes the normal four-view domain with the hard-mild domain and applies strong-noise augmentation during training.

## 6. Results

### 6.1 Single-view full-stack versus single-gate baselines

On the normalized single-view dataset, the full three-gate stack achieves the highest mean accuracy under clean, light-noise, and strong-noise evaluation. The independent evaluation accuracies are:

| condition | full stack | gate0 | gate1 | gate2 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| clean | 0.9697 | 0.9091 | 0.7121 | 0.8636 |
| light noise | 0.9545 | 0.8636 | 0.5151 | 0.7727 |
| strong noise | 0.7424 | 0.6970 | 0.5152 | 0.5152 |

These results show that the complete gate stack contains useful information beyond any individual gate in the single-view controlled setting.

Figure 5. Independent evaluation of the full three-gate stack and single-gate baselines under clean, light-noise, and strong-noise conditions. Bars denote mean accuracy over three random seeds (42/332/2026), and error bars denote standard deviation. The full three-gate stack gives the highest mean accuracy in all three evaluation conditions, supporting the usefulness of multi-gate information beyond a single selected range slice. Source file: `writing/figures/fig5_full_stack_vs_single_gate_robustness.png`.

### 6.2 Fusion-mode comparison

The fusion comparison shows that the best fusion strategy depends on the evaluation condition. Attention-residual fusion is strong under clean single-view validation, but attention fusion is more stable under strong noise. Therefore, the paper does not claim a novel or universally optimal fusion head.

Figure 6. Independent evaluation of three full-stack fusion strategies under clean, light-noise, and strong-noise conditions. Bars denote mean accuracy over three random seeds, and error bars denote standard deviation. The attention-residual head provides the best clean-condition performance, mean fusion is slightly better under light noise, while attention fusion is more robust under strong noise. This indicates that gate-stack fusion is a design variable rather than a solved component. Source file: `writing/figures/fig6_full_stack_fusion_robustness.png`.

### 6.3 Four-view grouped validation

The four-view dataset tests whether the gate-stack advantage remains when each source model is rendered from four yaw orientations and all views of the same source model are grouped during splitting. The full three-gate stack remains the best mean performer:

|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|

The strong-noise margin is especially important: under stronger degradation, the complete gate stack remains substantially more stable than any single gate.

### 6.5 Hard-nuisance failure boundary

The clean/noisy-trained four-view attention models fail under structured hard-nuisance shifts:

| condition | full stack | gate0 | gate1 | gate2 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| hard nuisance v2 | 0.5000 | 0.4697 | 0.5000 | 0.4545 |
| hard nuisance v3 mild | 0.5000 | 0.4848 | 0.5000 | 0.4545 |

This is a failure-boundary result, not a negative result to hide. It shows that clean/noisy validation does not imply robustness to structured reflectance, background, and occlusion shifts. Therefore, nuisance-aware simulation must be treated as part of the imaging model.

Figure 9. Failure-boundary evaluation under structured nuisance shifts. The four-view v8 attention models are trained on the normalized clean/noisy dataset and evaluated on clean, light-noise, strong-noise, and hard-nuisance datasets. The hard-nuisance datasets introduce low-frequency reflectance texture, weak background scatter, and partial occlusion while preserving per-sample maximum intensity. The full gate stack remains robust under the original clean/noisy protocol but collapses to approximately chance accuracy under structured nuisance shifts, indicating that nuisance-aware simulation and training are required before making realistic robustness claims. Source file: `writing/figures/fig9_hard_nuisance_failure_boundary.png`.

### 6.6 Domain-randomized training improves the robustness boundary

To address the hard-nuisance failure, a domain-mixed dataset is constructed by combining the normal four-view domain with the hard-mild domain:

```text
dataset_new/Military_TF_v8_mv4_norm_plus_hardv3_mild
```

The domain-mixed model is trained with strong-noise augmentation. Compared with normal mixed-noise training and online nuisance augmentation, explicit domain mixing provides the best hard-nuisance robustness:

| strategy | clean | light noise | strong noise | hard mild | hard strong |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| normal mixaug | 0.9848 | 0.9811 | 0.9205 | 0.5000 | 0.5000 |
| online nuisance | 0.9697 | 0.7879 | 0.5606 | 0.5000 | 0.5000 |
| domain mix + strong noise | 0.9394 | 0.9242 | 0.9091 | 0.9394 | 0.7727 |

The domain-mixed model sacrifices some clean and light-noise accuracy but substantially improves the hard-nuisance boundary. This supports a manuscript-safe claim: explicit domain-randomized simulation improves structured-nuisance robustness in the current simulation setting.

Figure 10. Training strategy comparison under clean/noisy evaluation and structured hard-nuisance shifts. Bars denote mean grouped-validation accuracy over three random seeds, and error bars denote standard deviation. The normal mixed-noise baseline performs best under clean and light-noise conditions but collapses to chance accuracy under the hard-nuisance datasets. Online structured nuisance augmentation does not recover hard-nuisance performance in the current implementation and weakens strong-noise robustness. In contrast, explicit domain mixing between the normal and hard-mild simulated domains, combined with strong-noise augmentation, recovers hard-mild accuracy to 0.9394 and hard-strong accuracy to 0.7727 while preserving 0.9091 strong-noise accuracy. Source file: `writing/figures/fig10_domain_randomization_strategy_comparison.png`.

## 7. Discussion

The current evidence supports three main conclusions.

First, the gated-image stack provides more stable evidence than individual gate images in the controlled simulation. This conclusion is supported by both single-view and four-view grouped validation. The four-view result is especially important because it reduces the possibility that a fixed viewpoint alone explains the result.

Second, anti-shortcut validation is necessary. The raw simulation contains a strong scalar brightness shortcut, and direct classification on such data would be misleading. Per-gate maximum normalization reduces this shortcut, but single-gate images remain informative. Therefore, scalar diagnostics and single-gate neural ablations are both needed.

Third, robustness must be evaluated beyond clean/noisy perturbations. The hard-nuisance boundary shows that normal mixed-noise training collapses under structured reflectance, background, and occlusion shifts. Domain-randomized training improves this boundary, but the result remains simulation-domain evidence rather than proof of real-world decoy robustness.

The neural model should be interpreted as a lightweight baseline. The experiments show that fusion design affects robustness, but they do not support a claim that a particular attention-residual design is the main innovation. The stronger and more defensible contribution is the physical simulation and validation chain.

## 8. Limitations

This study remains limited in several ways. The dataset contains only 44 manually selected source models, so the results should be treated as controlled validation rather than large-scale military recognition. The viewpoint protocol includes four yaw rotations but not broad elevation changes or realistic sensor-placement variation. The physical model uses simplified rectangular pulse and gate windows and does not fully model detector response, atmospheric propagation, material BRDF, timing jitter, or real backgrounds. The planar false target is also idealized: it preserves the visible silhouette and removes depth, whereas real decoys may include folds, supports, nonuniform reflectance, deformation, and environmental clutter.

The domain-randomized result improves the simulated robustness boundary but does not prove real-world robustness. The completed domain-mixed single-gate ablation further supports the gate-stack claim, but it remains limited to the current simulated datasets and selected nuisance types.

## 9. Conclusion

This work presents a physics-interpretable gated-imaging simulation and anti-shortcut validation framework for discriminating true 3D targets from planar false targets. True targets are simulated through depth-dependent three-gate responses, while planar false targets are generated by camera-depth flattening and rectangular pulse-gate overlap modulation. The experiments show that raw simulated images can contain strong brightness shortcuts and that anti-shortcut controls are required before neural accuracy can be interpreted physically. After normalization and grouped validation, the full three-gate stack outperforms single-gate baselines on average under clean, light-noise, and strong-noise evaluation. Structured hard-nuisance tests reveal a failure boundary for clean/noisy-trained models, while explicit domain-randomized simulation improves this boundary. The final domain-mixed single-gate ablation confirms that the full gate stack remains the strongest mean performer across all five evaluation conditions. The current results support the value of gate-stack observations and domain-randomized simulation in a controlled setting, and they provide a foundation for broader viewpoint validation, larger datasets, real-system calibration, and future multimodal optical-neural target recognition.
